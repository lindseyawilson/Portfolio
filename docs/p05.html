Another question: how have age gaps in movies changed over time? I can imagine that really huge age gaps might have been more common in the past than they are today, and it would be interesting to visualize that change over time:

movies %>%
  ggplot(aes(x = Release.Year, y = Age.Difference)) + 
  geom_jitter() +
  geom_smooth(method = "lm")
## `geom_smooth()` using formula = 'y ~ x'

It looks like I was right! To follow down this road, letâ€™s put release year into a linear model that predicts age difference and see how it does:

m_diff_year <- linear_reg() %>%
  set_engine("lm") %>%
  fit(Age.Difference ~ Release.Year, data = movies)

tidy(m_diff_year)
## # A tibble: 2 Ã— 5
##   term         estimate std.error statistic  p.value
##   <chr>           <dbl>     <dbl>     <dbl>    <dbl>
## 1 (Intercept)   229.      29.5         7.76 1.80e-14
## 2 Release.Year   -0.109    0.0148     -7.41 2.43e-13
glance(m_diff_year)$r.squared
## [1] 0.04522973

So we do have a somewhat consistent relationship between release year and age difference. For every year that passes, the average age gap between leads is predicted to decrease by 0.11 years, and this model explains about 4% of the variance in age gap. The p-value for that slope is also extremely significant (p = 2.43e-13), so we can be confident itâ€™s a real effect.

I want to see if we can explain more variance in age gap by adding the gender of the older person in the pair to the model. My gut intuition is that movies where the man is older have larger age gaps than movies where the woman is older:

Looks like I wasnâ€™t quite right. Descriptively, the older member of the pair being a woman does predict a smaller age gap, but neither this slope nor the interaction term are significant. Additionally, the adjusted R-squared basically doesnâ€™t increase at all, so weâ€™re clearly not explaining much more variance.

Finally, letâ€™s replace the gender of the older member of the pair with the age of the older member of the pair. This model is bound to increase the amount of variance we can explain; large age gaps are only possible when one member of the pair is older:
